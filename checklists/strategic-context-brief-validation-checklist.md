# Strategic Context Brief Validation Checklist

## Purpose

Validate that a completed Strategic Context Brief contains all essential context and discovery insights to serve as the handoff document from Discovery to DEFINE phase, enabling the Strategic Analyst to begin work with clear understanding of situation.

## What Gets Validated

**Template Reference:** `templates/strategic-context-brief.md`

**Used In:** Discovery phase as handoff to DEFINE phase

**Audience:** Strategic Analyst beginning DEFINE phase work

## Blocking Criteria (All Required)

### 1. Business Context is Clear ‚úì

- [ ] **Organization and industry understood**
  - What business is the organization in?
  - What's the industry/market?
  - What's organization size, structure, stage?
  - What's competitive landscape overview?

- [ ] **Current situation documented**
  - Current state of business (growth, challenges, opportunities)
  - Financial position (if relevant)
  - Market position (leader, challenger, niche)
  - Strategic priorities and constraints

- [ ] **Context sufficient for analysis**
  - Would analyst understand business without more conversation?
  - Are key industry dynamics noted?
  - Are organizational capabilities understood?
  - Are strategic constraints visible?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
ORGANIZATION:
SaaS company, Series B stage, $15M ARR, 80 employees
Enterprise-focused market positioning
Geographic focus: US and Europe
Product: Integration platform for enterprise data management

INDUSTRY:
Enterprise data integration market, $10B+ TAM
Growing at 20% annually
Consolidating around platform leaders
Key dynamics: Cloud adoption, API-first architecture, integration complexity

MARKET POSITION:
We're #3 player in enterprise segment (#1 = Competitor X, #2 = Competitor Y)
Differentiated by ease-of-use and integration breadth
Premium pricing strategy ($10K-50K+ annual contracts)
Strong enterprise customer success (NPS 75+, 95% retention)

STRATEGIC CONSTRAINTS:
Limited R&D resources (10 engineers for core product)
Distributed sales team (need to expand for growth)
Competing for enterprise attention (need product differentiation)
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Complete market analysis
- ‚ùå Detailed financial data
- ‚ùå Full organizational chart
- ‚ùå Comprehensive competitive intelligence

### 2. Challenge/Opportunity is Articulated ‚úì

- [ ] **What is being addressed is clear**
  - Is it a problem or opportunity?
  - What is the business impact?
  - Why does it matter now?
  - What's the context triggering the work?

- [ ] **Problem/opportunity is specific**
  - What specifically is happening?
  - Who is affected?
  - What's the scope?
  - What's at stake?

- [ ] **Initial perspective documented**
  - What does user think the problem is?
  - What are they hoping to understand?
  - What are their concerns?
  - What would success look like?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
CHALLENGE:
We're experiencing 15% annual churn in our SMB segment, representing
$5M in lost ARR. Meanwhile, competitors are moving aggressively
downmarket with simplified, lower-cost offerings. We need to understand
whether to defend our enterprise-only positioning or pursue the SMB
market opportunity.

BUSINESS IMPACT:
- Direct: $5M annual revenue at risk from SMB churn
- Strategic: Potential competitive vulnerability if SMB segment grows
- Opportunity: $2B SMB market opportunity (if we choose to pursue)

TIMING:
We're profitable and stable in enterprise. Decision needed in next
quarter before competitive window closes and resources needed to execute.

USER'S PERSPECTIVE:
CEO believes we should explore SMB opportunity but has concerns about
organizational distraction and execution risk. VP Sales thinks we need
to fix SMB pricing/positioning. Product team concerned about feature
complexity vs. SMB simplicity needs.

SUCCESS CRITERIA (INITIAL):
Clear understanding of SMB churn drivers
Quantified market opportunity assessment
Strategic recommendation on market approach
Execution roadmap if pursuing SMB strategy
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Root cause analysis
- ‚ùå Market research
- ‚ùå Detailed problem breakdown
- ‚ùå Stakeholder consensus

### 3. Stakeholder Landscape Mapped ‚úì

- [ ] **Key stakeholders identified**
  - Decision makers
  - Key influencers
  - Affected parties
  - Subject matter experts

- [ ] **Stakeholder perspectives captured**
  - What does each stakeholder care about?
  - What are their concerns?
  - What are their constraints?
  - Are there conflicts or differing views?

- [ ] **Engagement approach noted**
  - Who needs to be engaged in analysis?
  - Who needs to approve recommendations?
  - Who will implement decisions?
  - Communication and buy-in strategy?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
KEY STAKEHOLDERS:

DECISION MAKERS:
- CEO (strategic direction decision authority)
  Concerns: Execution risk, organizational distraction
  Position: Interested in SMB but wants data-driven decision

- Board (if strategic decision with capital implications)
  Concerns: Market opportunity, competitive risk, financial returns
  Position: Support growth opportunity if risk acceptable

OPERATIONAL LEADERSHIP:
- VP Product (product strategy, roadmap impact)
  Concerns: Feature complexity, product vision
  Position: Skeptical of SMB; prefers focus

- VP Sales (GTM strategy, sales capability)
  Concerns: Different sales motion, team capability
  Position: Believes SMB is urgent; we're losing customers

- CFO (financial impact, investment)
  Concerns: Profitability, capital allocation
  Position: Will fund if ROI justifies

SUBJECT MATTER EXPERTS:
- Head of Customer Success (churn insight, customer needs)
  Input: Exit interview themes, customer feedback
- Sales team (competitive intelligence, customer feedback)
  Input: Lost deal analysis, competitive positioning

ENGAGEMENT APPROACH:
- Initial discovery: CEO, VP Sales, VP Product, Head of CS (input)
- Analysis phase: Finance review, cross-functional interviews
- Recommendation: Executive team discussion and decision
- Implementation: Operational leaders lead execution
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Complete stakeholder mapping
- ‚ùå Detailed influence analysis
- ‚ùå Change management strategy
- ‚ùå Consensus building

### 4. Assumptions & Unknowns Documented ‚úì

- [ ] **Key assumptions about problem stated**
  - What are we assuming to be true?
  - Why do we believe it?
  - What evidence supports it?
  - What would prove us wrong?

- [ ] **Known unknowns identified**
  - What don't we know?
  - What would be important to learn?
  - What's the priority of unknowns?
  - How will we learn these?

- [ ] **Analysis questions framed**
  - What are the key questions to answer?
  - Are questions specific and answerable?
  - Would answers inform decisions?
  - Are questions prioritized?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
KEY ASSUMPTIONS:

Assumption 1: SMB churn is driven by pricing/feature misalignment
Evidence: Customer feedback in exit interviews; competitive positioning
Confidence: MODERATE (anecdotal; need data validation)
What would prove wrong: If other factors (support, brand) are primary drivers

Assumption 2: SMB market opportunity is substantial
Evidence: Market research reports; customer inquiry patterns
Confidence: MODERATE (market research can be dated; need validation)
What would prove wrong: If market is smaller or growing slower than expected

KNOWN UNKNOWNS:

Unknown 1: What specifically drives SMB churn? (Price? Features? Support?)
Why important: Determines strategic response options
How to learn: Exit interviews, cohort analysis, competitive analysis
Priority: CRITICAL (fundamental to decision)

Unknown 2: What is SMB market demand/TAM for our solution?
Why important: Determines opportunity size
How to learn: Market research, customer interviews, TAM analysis
Priority: CRITICAL

Unknown 3: Can we profitably serve both enterprise and SMB?
Why important: Determines feasibility of dual-segment strategy
How to learn: Unit economics analysis, business modeling
Priority: HIGH

Unknown 4: Would our current enterprise customers object to SMB focus?
Why important: Determines execution risk
How to learn: Customer advisory interviews, sentiment analysis
Priority: MEDIUM

ANALYSIS QUESTIONS:

Critical Questions:
1. Why are SMB customers churning? (root causes)
2. How large is the SMB market opportunity? (TAM/SAM/SOM)
3. What do competitors offer that we don't? (competitive gaps)
4. Can we profitably serve both segments? (unit economics)

Supporting Questions:
5. What would SMB customers need differently? (product requirements)
6. How would enterprise customers react? (retention risk)
7. What's the competitive response timeline? (market window)
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå All unknowns identified
- ‚ùå Complete assumption validation
- ‚ùå All analysis questions answered
- ‚ùå Perfect assumption clarity

### 5. Discovery Insights Documented ‚úì

- [ ] **Key discovery findings captured**
  - What did discovery conversation reveal?
  - What surprised us?
  - What themes emerged?
  - What points to investigate?

- [ ] **Customer/stakeholder voice included**
  - What did users/customers say?
  - What are they most concerned about?
  - What patterns emerged?
  - Are quotes/examples provided?

- [ ] **Red flags or opportunities noted**
  - What should we watch for?
  - What opportunities emerged?
  - What concerns were raised?
  - What areas need focus?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
KEY DISCOVERY FINDINGS:

Finding 1: SMB Customer Dissatisfaction Pattern
- Multiple SMB customers mentioned pricing as primary concern
- Described our solution as "enterprise-grade for SMB needs"
- Expressed frustration with feature complexity
- Noted competitors offer simpler, cheaper alternatives
Implication: SMB market willing to trade advanced features for simplicity/price

Finding 2: Enterprise Customer Strength
- Enterprise customers express high satisfaction
- Specifically value integration breadth and customization
- NPS scores are strong (75+)
- Long-term contracts and expansion deals common
Implication: Enterprise positioning is winning; don't lose focus

Finding 3: Market Dynamics Shift
- Multiple sources mentioned competitor downmarket moves
- Customer advisory board flagged competitive threat in SMB
- Competitive landscape consolidating around two tiers
Implication: Window for SMB entry may be time-limited

Finding 4: Product-Market Fit Questions
- SMB customers asking for simplified feature sets
- Enterprise customers want advanced features and customization
- Same product serving both seems challenging
Implication: May need separate product/positioning for SMB

CUSTOMER VOICE:

Quote from Churned SMB Customer:
"You're great for enterprise, but you're overkill for what we need. We'd
pay $500/month, not $5K/month. We found a competitor that gives us 80%
of the features at 20% of the cost."

Quote from Enterprise Customer:
"Your integration ecosystem is why we chose you. We need 15+ integrations
to work seamlessly. Most of your competitors can't do this."

Quote from Sales Team:
"We're losing SMB deals to Competitor Z on price every week. They're not
as capable, but they're 'good enough' and cheap. We can't compete on price
with our current positioning."

RED FLAGS:

Flag 1: Organizational capability concern
- Building/supporting dual-segment business is different from
  current enterprise focus
- Team has enterprise DNA; SMB is different
- Execution risk is real

Flag 2: Timing concern
- Market window for SMB entry estimated at 12-18 months
- Competitive moves happening now
- Decision timeline is critical

OPPORTUNITIES:

Opportunity 1: Leverage enterprise strength
- Our enterprise success is competitive advantage in SMB
- We could position as "enterprise-grade reliability for SMB simplicity"
- Reference customers could help SMB sales

Opportunity 2: Build integration ecosystem for SMB
- SMB increasingly wants integrations
- If we could provide 10-15 key integrations at SMB price point,
  would be compelling
- Different from enterprise integration depth
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Complete discovery notes
- ‚ùå All customer interviews transcribed
- ‚ùå Exhaustive stakeholder feedback
- ‚ùå Perfect organization of insights

### 6. Next Phase Readiness ‚úì

- [ ] **Analyst knows what to focus on**
  - Clear priority questions for analysis
  - Clear scope of analysis needed
  - Clear success criteria for phase
  - Clear what would be "enough"

- [ ] **Resources and access are arranged**
  - What data will analyst need?
  - Who is available for interviews?
  - What documentation exists?
  - Any constraints noted?

- [ ] **Analyst is prepared to begin**
  - Would analyst understand situation?
  - Do they know context and constraints?
  - Do they have stakeholder landscape?
  - Are expectations clear?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
FOCUS AREAS FOR ANALYSIS:

Priority 1: Root Cause Analysis (Why are SMB customers churning?)
- Conduct detailed exit interviews (15-20 customers)
- Analyze churn patterns by cohort, feature usage, pricing tier
- Compare churn reasons to competitor offerings
- Quantify impact of each driver (price, features, support)

Priority 2: Market Opportunity Assessment (How big is SMB market?)
- Validate TAM estimates through multiple sources
- Assess our addressable market (SAM) based on positioning
- Model our potential capture (SOM) based on realistic assumptions
- Compare to enterprise market opportunity

Priority 3: Competitive Analysis (What do competitors offer?)
- Create feature comparison matrix
- Assess competitive positioning and pricing
- Identify gaps we could exploit
- Assess competitive threats

Priority 4: Viability Assessment (Can we serve both profitably?)
- Build unit economics for SMB vs. enterprise
- Model dual-segment scenario financially
- Identify capability gaps
- Assess execution feasibility

AVAILABLE RESOURCES:

Data Resources:
- CRM system: Customer data, deal history, pricing, retention
- Customer interviews: Willing to participate (14 available)
- Exit interview notes: From past 6 months of SMB churn
- Sales intelligence: Competitive deal analysis, lost deal info

People Resources:
- CEO: Available for key interviews and context
- VP Sales: Available for competitive intelligence
- Head of CS: Has exit interview data and customer insights
- Finance team: Can provide financial analysis if needed

Access:
- CRM data: Full access available
- Customer interviews: CS team can coordinate; expect 1-2 weeks
- Board materials: If needed for context
- Competitive data: Sales has some intelligence gathered

CONSTRAINTS:
- Analysis window: 4-6 weeks (decision needed by end of quarter)
- SMB customer contact: Small number (50 customers); limited sample size
- Competitive data: Mostly public + sales intelligence (not comprehensive)
- Resource availability: CEO limited (quarterly planning other priority)

SUCCESS CRITERIA FOR DEFINE PHASE:

Minimum:
- Root causes of SMB churn identified and quantified
- Market opportunity sized (TAM/SAM/SOM)
- Competitive landscape understood
- Strategic choice options articulated

Ideal:
- Above plus
- Unit economics modeled for SMB vs. enterprise
- Execution feasibility assessed
- Stakeholder alignment beginning to form
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Complete resource specifications
- ‚ùå Detailed project plan
- ‚ùå All data sources mapped
- ‚ùå Perfect timeline for analysis

## Quality Checks

Before confirming brief is complete, ask yourself:

1. **Would Analyst understand situation without more conversation?**
   - Business context clear?
   - Challenge understood?
   - Stakeholder landscape visible?
   - Next steps obvious?

2. **Does Analyst have what they need to begin?**
   - Key questions defined?
   - Analysis priorities clear?
   - Resources identified?
   - Success criteria articulated?

3. **Is brief credible?**
   - Based on discovery conversation?
   - Reflects stakeholder perspectives?
   - Includes customer voice?
   - Balanced (not biased)?

4. **Is context preserved?**
   - Could someone 6 months later understand?
   - Are assumptions documented?
   - Are sources noted?
   - Is discovery captured?

## Common Issues & Resolution

| Issue | Signal | Resolution |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Too detailed** | "20 pages of notes" | Summarize; focus on essentials |
| **Too vague** | "Unclear what we're analyzing" | Be more specific and concrete |
| **Missing context** | "Who are the stakeholders?" | Map stakeholder landscape |
| **No discovery captured** | "What did we learn?" | Document key findings and themes |
| **Not actionable** | "What should Analyst do?" | Define clear priority questions |
| **Biased perspective** | "Reads like advocacy" | Include multiple stakeholder views |

## Post-Validation Actions

Once this checklist passes:

1. **Share with DEFINE Analyst**
   - Brief is provided at phase start
   - Analyst reviews and confirms understanding
   - Clarify any questions
   - Analyst confirms ready to begin

2. **Establish Success Criteria**
   - Confirm what "complete DEFINE" looks like
   - Establish completion timeline
   - Note dependencies or constraints
   - Plan phase completion review

3. **Prepare Handoff**
   - Archive discovery context
   - Prepare for phase transition
   - Brief leadership on progress
   - Plan next phase logistics

4. **Keep for Reference**
   - Save as reference throughout project
   - Update if context changes
   - Reference in final synthesis
   - Use for learning/reflection
