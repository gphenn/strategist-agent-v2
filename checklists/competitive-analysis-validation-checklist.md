# Competitive Analysis Validation Checklist

## Purpose

Validate that a completed Competitive Analysis template contains all essential competitive intelligence with sufficient depth and structure to inform strategic decision-making.

## What Gets Validated

**Template Reference:** `templates/competitive-analysis.md`

**Used In:** ANALYSE phase for competitive landscape understanding

**Audience:** Strategy team, product leadership, marketing, executives understanding competitive positioning

## Blocking Criteria (All Required)

### 1. Competitor Identification & Scoping âœ“

- [ ] **All direct competitors identified**
  - Who are 3-5 main direct competitors?
  - How are they defined as direct (competing for same customers)?
  - Market share estimates provided
  - Geographic/segment scope of each

- [ ] **Indirect competitors and substitutes included**
  - What alternative solutions exist?
  - Who competes for same customer need (different approach)?
  - Build-vs-buy considerations documented
  - Why considered indirect (addressed different need)?

- [ ] **Competitive scope is clear**
  - What market segment is analyzed?
  - What geographies included?
  - What customer types?
  - Time period of analysis documented

**What This Looks Like:**
```
DIRECT COMPETITORS (compete for same customers):
1. Competitor X (Market Share: 40%)
   - Enterprise-focused, premium pricing, full-featured
   - Geographic presence: US, Europe, APAC
   - Target segment: Enterprise 500+ employees

2. Competitor Y (Market Share: 25%)
   - Mid-market focused, moderate pricing, balanced feature set
   - Geographic presence: US, Europe
   - Target segment: Mid-market 100-500 employees

3. Competitor Z (Market Share: 15%)
   - SMB-focused, low pricing, simplified features
   - Geographic presence: US only
   - Target segment: SMB <100 employees

INDIRECT COMPETITORS (alternative solutions):
- In-house development (build vs. buy)
- Spreadsheet-based solutions (manual processes)
- Legacy enterprise systems (switching costs)
- Open-source alternatives (free but limited support)

SCOPE:
Analysis focuses on US market, all company sizes, 2024-2025
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- âŒ Every possible competitor
- âŒ Global coverage (can focus on key markets)
- âŒ Complete market research
- âŒ Exact market share data (estimates okay)

### 3. Competitive Positioning & Differentiation âœ“

- [ ] **Market positioning of each competitor clear**
  - How is each positioned? (Premium? Value? Niche?)
  - Target customer profile for each
  - Value proposition of each
  - Positioning evolving? (trends?)

- [ ] **Competitive differentiation identified**
  - What makes us different?
  - What are sustainable advantages?
  - What's defensible vs. easily copied?
  - What are competitor advantages?

- [ ] **Market dynamics understood**
  - Are competitors consolidating or fragmenting?
  - Are new competitors entering?
  - Are any leaving/declining?
  - What's driving market dynamics?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
COMPETITIVE POSITIONING:

US (Enterprise-Focused Premium):
- Positioning: "Enterprise-grade platform for complex integration needs"
- Value Prop: Advanced features, customization, dedicated support
- Target: Enterprise 500+ employees, complex technical requirements
- Differentiation: Integration ecosystem (90+ pre-built connectors)

Competitor X (Enterprise Premium):
- Positioning: "Most powerful platform; market leader"
- Value Prop: Full-featured, industry recognition, market depth
- Target: Enterprise Fortune 500 / largest deployments
- Differentiation: Feature breadth, market dominance

Competitor Z (SMB Value):
- Positioning: "Simple, affordable solution for growing companies"
- Value Prop: Easy to use, affordable, quick deployment
- Target: SMB <100 employees, non-technical users
- Differentiation: Simplicity, price point

MARKET DYNAMICS:
- Consolidation around two tiers: Premium (Comp X) and Value (Comp Z)
- New entrant: Comp Y emerging as mid-market specialist
- Market growing 25% annually; features commoditizing
- Trend: Movement toward vertical specialization and ease-of-use
```

### 4. Competitive Threats & Vulnerabilities âœ“

- [ ] **Competitive threats identified**
  - What are competitors doing well that threatens us?
  - Are competitors moving into our segments?
  - Are new competitors emerging?
  - What competitive moves are likely?

- [ ] **Our vulnerabilities understood**
  - What would competitors exploit?
  - Where are we exposed?
  - What would hurt us most?
  - How likely are these attacks?

- [ ] **Our competitive advantages assessed**
  - What can competitors not easily copy?
  - What's our defensible moat?
  - What would take competitors years to build?
  - How sustainable are our advantages?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
COMPETITIVE THREATS:

Threat 1: Competitor Z moving upmarket
- They're adding enterprise features; could take mid-market
- Likelihood: HIGH | Impact: MEDIUM | Timeline: 12 months
- Our vulnerability: Positioned as premium, not accessible to mid-market
- Response: Develop mid-market pricing tier; reduce complexity

Threat 2: Competitor X launching SMB product
- They could leverage brand and existing customer base
- Likelihood: MEDIUM | Impact: HIGH | Timeline: 18 months
- Our vulnerability: SMB is new; brand not established there
- Response: Establish strong SMB positioning before they enter

OUR COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES:

Advantage 1: Integration Ecosystem (DEFENSIBLE)
- 90+ pre-built connectors competitors don't have
- Took us 3+ years to build; competitors 2+ years to copy
- Creates switching costs; hard to replicate
- Moat: STRONG (sustainable 2-3 years)

Advantage 2: Enterprise Customer Success Record (DEFENSIBLE)
- Track record with Fortune 500 companies
- Reference customers competitors can't easily get
- Trust advantage in enterprise
- Moat: MEDIUM (can be overcome with one big customer win)

Advantage 3: Feature Breadth (NOT DEFENSIBLE)
- We have advanced features
- But competitors can build features if motivated
- Not a durable advantage
- Moat: WEAK (features commoditizing)
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- âŒ Every possible threat
- âŒ Detailed competitive responses
- âŒ Complete moat analysis
- âŒ Quantified probabilities

### 5. Market Gaps & Opportunities âœ“

- [ ] **Unmet customer needs identified**
  - What do customers want that competitors don't provide?
  - Are there underserved customer segments?
  - Are there use cases no competitor addresses well?
  - Where is customer dissatisfaction high?

- [ ] **Market opportunities clear**
  - What's the competitive advantage opportunity?
  - What could we do differently?
  - Where could we win against competitors?
  - What market space is under-served?

- [ ] **Opportunity implications understood**
  - If we pursue this opportunity, what happens?
  - Can we execute on it?
  - Would customers respond?
  - What resources required?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
MARKET GAPS:

Gap 1: No competitor has strong SMB offering + Enterprise strength
- Competitors choose: either premium OR value, not both
- Gap: No vendor can serve both well
- Opportunity: If we could serve both, we'd be unique

Gap 2: Integration ecosystem unmatched; competitors have none
- All competitors require custom integration work
- Gap: No competitor offers pre-built connectors
- Opportunity: Deepen integration advantage; build platform ecosystem

Gap 3: Ease-of-use not strong in premium segment
- Premium competitors (us, Comp X) are complex
- Value competitors (Comp Z) are easy but lack features
- Gap: No vendor is both powerful AND easy
- Opportunity: Simplify UX without losing power

OPPORTUNITY IMPLICATIONS:

Opportunity A: Serve SMB with simplified offering
- Requires: New product, separate team, different GTM
- Customer response: Likely HIGH (market demand exists)
- Resources: $5M+ investment; 18-month timeline
- Competitive impact: Could establish dual-segment leadership

Opportunity B: Build integration marketplace
- Requires: Partner recruitment, marketplace infrastructure
- Customer response: Likely HIGH (partners want to build on APIs)
- Resources: $2M+ investment; 12-month timeline
- Competitive impact: Strengthen defensible moat
```

### 6. Data Quality & Sources âœ“

- [ ] **Analysis sources are documented**
  - Where did information come from?
  - What's the recency of data?
  - Any limitations in data?
  - Confidence level indicated

- [ ] **Assessment methodology is transparent**
  - How were competitors evaluated?
  - What criteria were used?
  - Any bias in assessment acknowledged?
  - Would someone understand how analysis was done?

- [ ] **Confidence levels are stated**
  - What's high confidence? Why?
  - What's moderate confidence? Why?
  - What's exploratory/uncertain?
  - What would improve confidence?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
DATA SOURCES:

Public Information:
- Company websites (accessed November 2024)
- Product documentation and feature lists
- Public pricing pages
- Press releases and announcements
- Third-party review sites (G2, Capterra)
- Confidence: HIGH for public features; MODERATE for roadmap

Customer Intelligence:
- 5 customer interviews (our customers) about competitor offers
- 3 partner interviews about competitor integrations
- Sales team feedback from lost deals
- Confidence: MODERATE (anecdotal; small sample)

Competitive Assessment:
- Trial accounts in 3 competitor products
- Feature testing and evaluation
- Documentation review
- Confidence: HIGH for features; MEDIUM for UX/performance

Industry Research:
- Market research report (published August 2024, 3 months old)
- Industry analyst reports
- Confidence: MODERATE (secondary; may have aged)

LIMITATIONS:
- Pricing data is public list pricing (actual deals often different)
- Roadmap information based on announcements (not all transparent)
- Market data is 3 months old (market moving fast)
- Focus is US market (global dynamics may differ)
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- âŒ Perfect data provenance
- âŒ Every source documented
- âŒ Academic-level rigor
- âŒ Exhaustive confidence levels

## Enhancing Criteria (Improving Quality)

These items strengthen the analysis but aren't strictly blocking:

- [ ] **Customer perspective integrated**
  - How do customers view competitive options?
  - What matters most to customers?
  - Why do customers choose competitors?
  - How sticky is customer loyalty?

- [ ] **Pricing analysis detailed**
  - Competitive pricing models documented
  - Our price positioning relative to competitors
  - Pricing trends and changes
  - Customer price sensitivity by segment

- [ ] **Sales intelligence captured**
  - What do lost deals tell us about competitors?
  - What are competitors' strongest arguments?
  - What are their weakest points?
  - What would win customers from them?

- [ ] **Scenario planning included**
  - If Competitor X moves downmarket, what happens?
  - If Competitor Z launches enterprise offering?
  - If new player enters market?
  - How do we respond to each?

- [ ] **Trend analysis extended**
  - Is competitive landscape consolidating or fragmenting?
  - What new technologies are competitors adopting?
  - How are customer preferences shifting?
  - What's the likely evolution of market?

## Quality Checks (Before Approving)

Before confirming analysis is complete, ask yourself:

1. **Does this give us clear picture of competitive landscape?**
   - Can we see who we compete with?
   - Can we see where we're strong/weak?
   - Can we see market opportunities?
   - Would someone understand competitive situation?

2. **Would this inform strategic decisions?**
   - Would this help us choose market segments?
   - Would this inform product strategy?
   - Would this help us position against competitors?
   - Would this identify opportunities?

3. **Is the analysis credible?**
   - Are sources documented?
   - Are confidence levels transparent?
   - Would someone trust this analysis?
   - Are limitations acknowledged?

4. **Does the analysis connect to our problem?**
   - Does competitive analysis address why we have the problem?
   - Does it show opportunities?
   - Does it show threats?
   - Does it inform strategic choices?

## Common Issues & Resolution

| Issue | Signal | Resolution |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Too many competitors** | "15 competitors listed" | Focus on 3-5 direct; others are indirect |
| **Features not comparable** | "Can't tell who wins" | Create matrix; use common criteria |
| **Positioning unclear** | "I don't know where we stand" | Define each competitor's positioning clearly |
| **Threats missing** | "What should worry us?" | Ask what would hurt us most |
| **Opportunities vague** | "Where's the upside?" | Identify market gaps we could fill |
| **Data too old** | "From 2023" | Update with current information |

   - Establish tracking for competitive moves
   - Monitor competitor announcements
   - Update analysis quarterly

4. **Archive for reference**
   - Save competitive intelligence
   - Document date of analysis
   - Note sources and confidence
   - Reference in future strategic work
