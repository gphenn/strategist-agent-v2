# Competitive Benchmarking Quality Checklist

## Purpose

Validate that competitive benchmarking has selected appropriate comparables, identified meaningful gaps between us and competitors, and drawn actionable implications for strategy and positioning.

## What Gets Validated

**Task Reference:** `tasks/competitive-benchmarking.md`

**Used In:** ANALYSE phase for competitive intelligence and positioning analysis

**Audience:** Strategic analysts, product leaders, competitive strategists

---

## Blocking Criteria (All Required)

### 1. Comparables Are Appropriately Selected ‚úì

- [ ] **Comparable selection criteria are stated**
  - What makes a company "comparable"?
  - Why were these companies selected?
  - What makes them relevant to our strategy?
  - Are there other comparables we missed?

- [ ] **Direct competitors are included**
  - Who directly competes in our market?
  - Are major competitors represented?
  - Are emerging competitors included?
  - Have we covered competitive landscape?

- [ ] **Indirect/aspirational comparables included**
  - Are we benchmarking against adjacent competitors?
  - Are there companies we aspire to be like?
  - Are there "best-in-class" examples in other markets?
  - Why include indirect comparables?

**What This Looks Like:**

```
COMPARABLE SELECTION CRITERIA:

We are benchmarking against companies that:
1. Serve similar customer base (manufacturing companies)
2. Operate in similar market size ($100M-$1B+ revenue)
3. Have comparable business models (SaaS, recurring revenue)
4. Compete for customer attention and budget

SELECTED COMPARABLES:

DIRECT COMPETITORS (Same market):
1. CompetitorA (supply chain optimization, $250M revenue)
   - Established 2016; public company
   - Serves manufacturers globally
   - Comparable: Direct market competitor

2. CompetitorB (demand planning, $150M revenue)
   - Established 2015; venture-backed
   - Serves mid-to-large manufacturers
   - Comparable: Direct competitor, but narrower scope

3. CompetitorC (AI supply chain, $20M revenue)
   - Established 2020; emerging player
   - Focused on innovation approach
   - Comparable: Emerging threat; different positioning

INDIRECT COMPETITORS (Adjacent markets/approaches):
4. Indirect1 (enterprise software for manufacturing, $500M revenue)
   - Broader platform; supply chain module
   - Could encroach on our market
   - Comparable: Potential future threat

5. Indirect2 (consulting + analytics services, $50M revenue)
   - Similar customer base; different delivery model
   - Could become product competitor
   - Comparable: Alternative solution approach

ASPIRATIONAL COMPARABLES (Best-in-class examples):
6. Aspirational1 (industry leader in vertical SaaS, $2B revenue)
   - Different industry; similar market dynamics
   - Demonstrates what dominant player looks like
   - Comparable: Shows scale/profitability potential

7. Aspirational2 (SaaS company with 90%+ retention, leader)
   - Different market; leadership in customer success
   - Shows what best-in-class retention looks like
   - Comparable: Benchmark for customer success excellence

RATIONALE:
- Direct competitors: Essential to understand market positioning
- Indirect competitors: Important to understand threats and alternatives
- Aspirational: Important to understand what excellence looks like
- Together: Complete competitive picture
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Every possible competitor
- ‚ùå Only exact same business model
- ‚ùå Only public companies (private OK)
- ‚ùå Perfect match on all dimensions

### 2. Benchmarking Dimensions Are Relevant ‚úì

- [ ] **Benchmarking dimensions are clearly defined**
  - What metrics are being compared?
  - Why were these metrics chosen?
  - Are they relevant to strategy?
  - Are they measurable/comparable?

- [ ] **Dimensions cover key competitive factors**
  - Product features/capability
  - Market positioning/messaging
  - Pricing and business model
  - Sales/GTM approach
  - Customer success/support
  - Financial metrics (if public)
  - Company culture/values

- [ ] **Dimensions are concrete and measurable**
  - "Product features" is too vague
  - "15+ workflows supported" is concrete
  - Metrics can be compared across companies
  - Data is accessible/obtainable

**What This Looks Like:**

```
BENCHMARKING DIMENSIONS:

PRODUCT CAPABILITY:
Dimension 1: Core Features Breadth
- Demand planning capability
- Inventory optimization capability
- Demand sensing capability
- Supplier collaboration capability
- Measurement: Yes/No for each; scope if yes
- Relevance: Core product functionality

Dimension 2: Implementation Speed
- Time from purchase to go-live
- Typical: 3-6 months for competitor A
- Typical: 1-2 months for competitor B
- Relevance: Time-to-value for customers

Dimension 3: AI/ML Sophistication
- Do they use AI/ML in core product?
- What algorithms used (if disclosed)?
- What's the learning model (rules-based vs. ML)?
- Relevance: Product differentiation

MARKET POSITIONING:
Dimension 4: Target Customer Profile
- Company size (SMB, mid-market, enterprise)
- Industry focus (horizontal vs. vertical)
- Geography served
- Relevance: Where they compete; our white space

Dimension 5: Positioning Statement
- How do they describe themselves?
- What's their value proposition?
- What problem do they solve?
- Relevance: Understand competitive positioning

PRICING & BUSINESS MODEL:
Dimension 6: Pricing Model
- Subscription, usage-based, hybrid?
- Price point/ACV
- Payment terms
- Relevance: Our pricing strategy

Dimension 7: Contract Terms
- Multi-year vs. annual
- Typical contract length
- Early termination clauses
- Relevance: Our go-to-market approach

SALES & GTM:
Dimension 8: Sales Model
- Direct sales, channel, self-serve?
- Sales team size
- Geographic coverage
- Relevance: Our sales model viability

CUSTOMER SUCCESS:
Dimension 9: Retention Metrics
- NPS or customer satisfaction score (if disclosed)
- Estimated churn rate (if disclosed)
- Support model (dedicated account manager, ticketing, etc.)
- Relevance: Customer stickiness; support expectations

FINANCIAL METRICS:
Dimension 10: Scale & Growth
- Revenue (if public)
- Growth rate
- Profitability
- Funding (if known)
- Relevance: How much capital needed; growth potential

DIMENSION SELECTION RATIONALE:
- Each dimension answers a strategic question
- Collectively they paint competitive picture
- All are measurable or obtainable
- All are relevant to strategy
- Not exhaustive (selected key dimensions)
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Exhaustive benchmarking matrix
- ‚ùå 50+ dimensions (10-15 is good)
- ‚ùå Perfect data quality
- ‚ùå All dimensions for all competitors

### 3. Competitive Data Is Well-Sourced ‚úì

- [ ] **Data sources are identified**
  - Where did each data point come from?
  - How recent is the data?
  - How reliable are sources?
  - Are multiple sources used?

- [ ] **Data gathering methods are documented**
  - Customer interviews?
  - Public information (websites, marketing)?
  - Industry reports?
  - Sales intelligence?
  - Product research (own testing)?

- [ ] **Data quality and confidence are assessed**
  - How confident in each data point?
  - What's the time freshness?
  - Are there gaps?
  - What caveats apply?

### 4. Competitive Gaps Are Identified ‚úì

- [ ] **Gaps between us and competitors are specific**
  - What exactly are we better at?
  - What exactly are competitors better at?
  - Are gaps quantified where possible?
  - Are gaps clearly articulated?

- [ ] **Gaps are analyzed for strategic significance**
  - Which gaps matter most to customers?
  - Which gaps are defensible?
  - Which gaps can we close?
  - Which gaps define our positioning?

- [ ] **Gap analysis reveals white space**
  - Are there market opportunities we can exploit?
  - Are there customer needs unfulfilled?
  - Are there segments underserved?
  - Are there positioning angles available?

**What This Looks Like:**

```
COMPETITIVE GAPS ANALYSIS:

PRODUCT CAPABILITY GAPS:

Gap 1: Implementation Speed
- Competitor A: 3-6 months typical
- Competitor B: 1-2 months typical
- Us (planned): 2-3 months target
- Strategic significance: MEDIUM-HIGH
  Why: Time-to-value matters; customers want fast ROI
  Customer impact: If we're 2 months vs. 4 months, that's competitive advantage
- Defensibility: MEDIUM (implementation is about process, not proprietary)
- Our advantage: Plan to simplify implementation through automation
- Action: Make fast implementation part of positioning

Gap 2: Feature Breadth
- Competitor A: 15+ workflows; comprehensive
- Competitor B: 8 workflows; focused
- Us (planned): 6 core workflows + roadmap for more
- Strategic significance: MEDIUM
  Why: Customers value having "everything," but may not use all
  Customer impact: Focused approach may be strength (simplicity) or weakness (lack of depth)
- Defensibility: HIGH (building 15 workflows takes time and capital)
- Our advantage: Can position "focused and powerful" vs. "comprehensive and complex"
- Action: Choose positioning carefully; don't try to match A on breadth

Gap 3: AI/ML Sophistication
- Competitor C: AI-native from ground up
- Competitor A: Some ML (secondary feature)
- Competitor B: Rules-based (no ML)
- Us (planned): Hybrid (some ML, not core)
- Strategic significance: MEDIUM
  Why: AI is hype; customers want better predictions
  Customer impact: If our forecasts are better, customer savings justify premium
- Defensibility: MEDIUM (ML can be improved over time; not permanent gap)
- Our advantage: Strong data science team; can close gap faster than others
- Action: Develop ML roadmap; show competitive path for catching up

PRICING GAPS:

Gap 4: Price Point
- Competitor A: $40-120K ACV (premium positioning)
- Competitor B: $25-75K ACV (mid-market)
- Us (planned): $30-60K ACV (mid-market positioning)
- Strategic significance: HIGH
  Why: Price signals positioning and affects market access
  Customer impact: Lower price makes us accessible; but may signal lower quality
- Defensibility: HIGH (once set, hard to change)
- Our advantage: Can undercut Competitor A; compete with B
- Action: Price competitively; support with value messaging

GTM GAPS:

Gap 5: Sales Coverage
- Competitor A: Global sales team (50+ reps)
- Competitor B: Regional sales team (8-10 reps)
- Us (planned): Vertical sales team (3-5 reps to start)
- Strategic significance: MEDIUM
  Why: Sales coverage determines market reach
  Customer impact: Limited reach means we serve focused segments first
- Defensibility: MEDIUM (build over time as revenue grows)
- Our advantage: Focused coverage in 3 verticals; deep relationships
- Action: Go deep with few reps; build network effects

SUPPORT GAPS:

Gap 6: Customer Success
- Competitor A: Dedicated account managers (1 per account)
- Competitor B: Shared account managers (1 per 5-10 accounts)
- Us (planned): Dedicated account managers for top customers; self-serve for others
- Strategic significance: MEDIUM
  Why: Good support drives retention and expansion
  Customer impact: Dedicated support drives loyalty; we can't match A immediately
- Defensibility: MEDIUM (support can be improved)
- Our advantage: Can start with dedicated support for landing customers; scale to hybrid
- Action: Focus on exceptional support for early customers; build reputation

STRATEGIC WHITE SPACE IDENTIFIED:

White Space 1: "SIMPLICITY + DEPTH"
Gap: Competitor A is complex (comprehensive); Competitor B is simple (limited)
Opportunity: Can we be "simple yet powerful"? Yes.
Customer need: Many want simplicity without sacrificing core functionality
Action: Positioning angle; product design principle

White Space 2: "VERTICAL EXPERTISE"
Gap: All competitors are horizontal (serve all verticals equally)
Opportunity: Can we be "built for automotive" or "built for pharma"?
Customer need: Yes; vertical-specific best practices are highly valued
Action: Vertical go-to-market; product differentiation (vertical features)

White Space 3: "PREDICTIVE VISIBILITY"
Gap: Focus on forecasting (Competitor B) vs. optimization (Competitor A)
Opportunity: Can we own "predictive visibility into supply chain"?
Customer need: Yes; visibility is foundational need across all customers
Action: Core positioning; feature emphasis; customer messaging

WHITE SPACE RECOMMENDATION:
Focus on "Vertical Expertise + Simplicity + Predictive Visibility"
This creates unique positioning not fully claimed by anyone.
- Vertical focus: Different from horizontal competitors
- Simplicity: Vs. Competitor A's complexity
- Predictive: Vs. Competitor B's limited scope
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Exhaustive gap analysis (key gaps sufficient)
- ‚ùå Ranking all gaps
- ‚ùå Quantified gap sizing
- ‚ùå Gap closure plans (timing can be separate)

### 5. Implications for Strategy Are Drawn ‚úì

- [ ] **Competitive insights translate to strategic choices**
  - What should we do differently based on gaps?
  - How should competitive insights shape positioning?
  - How should insights shape product roadmap?
  - How should insights shape pricing?

- [ ] **Recommendations are actionable**
  - Would a leader know what to do with this?
  - Are implications specific (not vague)?
  - Do recommendations connect to gaps?
  - Are trade-offs identified?

- [ ] **Alternative strategies are considered**
  - Could we compete differently?
  - Are multiple positioning options explored?
  - What are trade-offs between options?
  - Which option is recommended?

**What This Looks Like:**

```
STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS & RECOMMENDATIONS:

IMPLICATION 1: Product Positioning Strategy

Insight: Competitor A dominates with comprehensive breadth; Competitor B
wins with simplicity. Gap: "Focused depth" is unclaimed.

Strategic Choice Options:

Option A: "Match Competitor A" (Comprehensive)
- Build all 15+ workflows
- Position as "Everything you need"
- Go head-to-head on breadth
- Trade-off: Requires $50M+ capital; long development time
- Recommendation: NOT RECOMMENDED (wrong capital, wrong timeline)

Option B: "Match Competitor B" (Simple)
- Build 6-8 core workflows only
- Position as "Demand planning, done right"
- Compete on ease of use and speed
- Trade-off: Limits market; customers want more features later
- Recommendation: POSSIBLE (but commoditizes us; low moat)

Option C: "Vertical Expertise" (RECOMMENDED)
- Build 8-10 workflows
- Optimize workflows for specific verticals (automotive, pharma, food)
- Position as "Built for [vertical]"
- Trade-off: Limits addressable market to 3 verticals initially
- Recommendation: YES (creates differentiation; builds defensibility)

Recommended Strategy: Option C (Vertical Expertise)
Why:
‚úì Creates defensible positioning (not directly comparable to horizontals)
‚úì Allows focused product development (not everything to everyone)
‚úì Builds vertical brand and moat
‚úì Enables vertical account teams (better customer fit)
‚úì Can expand to adjacent verticals (scalable model)

IMPLICATION 2: Pricing Strategy

Insight: Price point signals positioning. Competitor A is premium ($40-120K);
Competitor B is mid-market ($25-75K). Gap: Can we be "premium within vertical"?

Strategic Choice Options:

Option A: "Undercut Competitor B" ($20-50K ACV)
- Position as "Great value"
- Win on price
- Trade-off: Signals "discount/lower quality"; attracts price-sensitive customers
- Recommendation: NOT RECOMMENDED (wrong message)

Option B: "Match Competitor B" ($25-75K ACV)
- Position as "Competitive pricing"
- Neutral positioning
- Trade-off: No pricing advantage; compete on features
- Recommendation: POSSIBLE (safe but not differentiating)

Option C: "Premium Within Vertical" ($50-100K ACV for vertical-specific)
- Position as "Vertical expertise commands premium pricing"
- Win on value, not price
- Trade-off: Requires strong vertical value prop; only works if customers see value
- Recommendation: YES (supports vertical positioning)

Recommended Strategy: Option C
Why:
‚úì Aligns with vertical expertise positioning
‚úì Supports premium perception ("vertical expert")
‚úì Funds vertical product development
‚úì Signals quality and focus (not cheap)
‚úì Customers willing to pay for vertical optimization

Pricing Decision: $60-90K ACV to start (premium vs. horizontal; reasonable vs. Competitor A)

IMPLICATION 3: Sales/GTM Strategy

Insight: Sales model must match positioning. Horizontal competitors need big
sales teams. Vertical positioning can work with smaller, specialized team.

Strategic Choice Options:

Option A: "Enterprise Sales Machine" (50+ reps)
- Large sales team; geographic coverage
- Position as "Everywhere"
- Trade-off: Requires significant capital; harder to scale
- Recommendation: NOT RECOMMENDED (wrong capital model)

Option B: "Channel Partners" (resellers)
- Distribute through partners; light sales team
- Position as "Accessible"
- Trade-off: Lose customer relationship; lower margins; harder to control
- Recommendation: POSSIBLE (but loses direct relationships)

Option C: "Vertical Account Teams" (3-5 reps per vertical)
- Specialized sales teams for each vertical
- Build deep customer relationships in verticals
- Trade-off: Requires vertical expertise; slower to expand geographies
- Recommendation: YES (supports vertical positioning; builds moats)

Recommended Strategy: Option C
Why:
‚úì Aligns with vertical expertise positioning
‚úì Reps build deep vertical knowledge (better selling)
‚úì Customers value specialized reps (understanding their business)
‚úì Smaller team = lower capital requirement
‚úì Foundation for vertical expansion

Sales Model: 3 vertical-specialized reps to start (automotive, pharma, food);
add reps as teams hit quota

IMPLICATION 4: Product Roadmap

Insight: Competition is moving on features (A adding more), AI (C emphasizing),
implementation speed (B winning). We need to be strategic about roadmap.

Year 1 Roadmap Implications:
- Priority 1: Core workflows (6) + vertical optimization (our differentiation)
- Priority 2: Fast implementation (beat B on speed)
- Priority 3: Basic ML (match C's positioning, but prove ours is better)
- Priority 4: Vertical-specific features (build moat in each vertical)
- Not priority: Matching A's breadth (not this year)

Resource Allocation:
- 60% to vertical optimization (our differentiation)
- 20% to fast implementation (customer expectation)
- 15% to ML/AI foundation (competitive positioning)
- 5% to adjacent features (roadmap for later expansion)

IMPLICATION 5: Brand & Messaging

Insight: Positioning must differentiate. "Vertical Expertise + Simplicity + Speed"
creates clear, unique brand.

Brand Positioning Statement:
"[Our company] is the supply chain optimization platform built specifically for
[vertical]. We combine deep vertical expertise with intuitive design and rapid
implementation to help [vertical] optimize their unique supply chain challenges.
Unlike horizontal platforms, we don't try to be everything to everyone. We're
focused on what matters most in [your vertical]."

Messaging Pillars:
1. Vertical Expertise (not horizontal generic)
2. Simplicity (not complex)
3. Speed (implementation and ROI)
4. Deep Understanding (vertical-specific features and workflows)

Competitive Positioning:
- vs. Competitor A: "Simpler + Faster + Focused" (not comprehensive)
- vs. Competitor B: "Deeper" (not just demand planning)
- vs. Competitor C: "Proven + Reliable" (not just innovative AI)

SUMMARY OF STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS:

‚úì Product: Vertical expertise (not horizontal breadth)
‚úì Pricing: Premium ($60-90K) within verticals
‚úì Sales: Specialized vertical teams (not large enterprise sales machine)
‚úì Roadmap: Vertical optimization (not feature breadth)
‚úì Brand: "Vertical expert + simple + fast"
‚úì Differentiation: Not matched by competitors (defensible)

This creates coherent, differentiated strategy with defensible moat.
```

## Enhancing Criteria (Improving Quality)

These items strengthen the benchmarking but aren't strictly blocking:

- [ ] **Customer perspective included**

  - What do customers value most?
  - Where do we win/lose deals?

  - What deals did we lose? To whom?
  - What were decision factors?
  - Are patterns identified?

- [ ] **Third-party validation**
  - Industry analyst perspective (Gartner, etc.)?
  - Review by external competitive expert?
  - Validation from customers on findings?
  - Any external perspective on positioning?

  - If Competitor A cuts prices, what happens?
  - If Competitor C proves AI works, what happens?

  - How robust is our strategy?

- [ ] **Update cadence established**
  - When will benchmarking be revisited?
  - What triggers update?
  - How will real-world data be incorporated?
  - Who owns ongoing updates?

## Quality Checks (Before Approving)

Before confirming benchmarking is complete, ask yourself:

1. **Are comparables truly comparable?**
   - Would someone accept these as fair comparisons?
   - Are there other companies we should include?
   - Are we comparing apples to apples?
   - Are differences in size/scope acknowledged?

2. **Is data credible?**
   - Would someone trust these sources?
   - Are gaps/limitations acknowledged?
   - Would skeptic question any data point?
   - Is confidence level appropriate?

3. **Do gaps lead to strategy?**
   - Does benchmarking change what we should do?
   - Are strategic implications clear?
   - Would leader feel confident acting on this?
   - Are recommendations defensible?

4. **Is positioning unique?**
   - Does our positioning differ from competitors?
   - Would customers see difference?
   - Would difference matter to customers?
   - Is difference defensible/sustainable?

## Common Issues & Resolution

| Issue | Signal | Resolution |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Wrong comparables** | "These aren't our competitors" | Reassess selection criteria; include right companies |
| **Too many dimensions** | "50 metrics to track" | Focus on 10-15 most strategic dimensions |
| **No clear data source** | "Where did this come from?" | Document sources for every data point |
| **Outdated data** | "This was 2 years ago" | Use recent data; note freshness for each point |
| **No strategic implication** | "So what?" | Connect gaps to strategy; articulate action items |
| **Vague positioning** | "We'll be different somehow" | Be specific about positioning; articulate vs. each competitor |
| **Ignoring advantages** | "We're worse than everyone" | Highlight our advantages; find white space |

## Post-Validation Actions

Once this checklist passes:

1. **Finalize Competitive Briefing**
   - Executive summary (clear, actionable)
   - Full benchmarking matrix
   - Detailed competitive profiles
   - Strategic implications
   - Supporting appendices

2. **Present to Leadership**
   - Review competitive landscape
   - Discuss positioning options
   - Get alignment on recommended strategy
   - Identify resource implications
   - Assign ownership

3. **Conduct Customer Validation**
   - Share insights with target customers
   - Ask which positioning resonates
   - Validate customer value perception
   - Refine messaging based on feedback
   - Build customer confidence in differentiation

4. **Develop Product/Marketing Plans**
   - Product roadmap aligned with positioning
   - Marketing messaging aligned with positioning
   - Sales process aligned with positioning
   - Pricing aligned with positioning
   - All coherent around chosen strategy

5. **Plan Ongoing Updates**
   - Schedule quarterly competitive reviews
   - Monitor competitor product updates
   - Track win/loss data
   - Adjust strategy as competitive landscape shifts
   - Update benchmarking with new insights
