# Strategic Brief Validation Checklist

## Purpose

Validate that a completed Strategic Brief contains all essential sections with sufficient depth and clarity to serve as the executive summary of strategic work. This checklist ensures the brief can stand alone as a comprehensive strategic document.

## What Gets Validated

**Template Reference:** `templates/strategic-brief-tmpl.md`

**Used In:** DEFINE, STORY, and DECIDE phases as final executive summary

**Audience:** Executives, board, key stakeholders who need complete strategic picture without reading full analysis

## Blocking Criteria (All Required)

### 1. Executive Summary ‚úì

- [ ] **Opening paragraph captures full strategic situation**
  - What's the business challenge or opportunity?
  - Why does it matter (business impact)?
  - What's the strategic question being addressed?
  - Can someone understand the situation from this alone?

- [ ] **1-2 page summary is scannable**
  - Bold headers break up text
  - Key findings highlighted
  - Not dense paragraphs (bullets where appropriate)
  - Length is truly brief (1-2 pages maximum)

- [ ] **Tone is executive-appropriate**
  - Professional and credible
  - Evidence-based (not speculative)
  - Action-oriented (not descriptive)
  - Clear and direct (not academic)

**What This Looks Like:**
```
SITUATION:
We're experiencing 15% annual customer churn in our SMB segment,
representing $5M in lost ARR. Competitors are moving aggressively
downmarket with simplified, lower-cost offerings. Simultaneously,
our enterprise segment is highly stable (95% retention, $15M ARR)
with strong growth potential.

STRATEGIC QUESTION:
Should we expand into the SMB segment with a separate offering,
or maintain our focus on enterprise excellence?

KEY FINDINGS:
- SMB churn is driven primarily by pricing (60%) and feature gaps (30%)
- Enterprise customers are highly satisfied and loyal
- Market opportunity in SMB is substantial ($2B TAM; 25% annual growth)
- Competitors are establishing SMB presence; window may be time-limited
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Complete detailed analysis
- ‚ùå All supporting data
- ‚ùå Every finding detailed
- ‚ùå Polished presentation formatting

---

### 2. Problem/Opportunity Statement ‚úì

- [ ] **Problem is clearly and specifically stated**
  - Not vague or abstract
  - Business impact is quantified
  - Scope boundaries are defined
  - Root causes are indicated (without full analysis)

- [ ] **Problem statement could be understood by anyone**
  - Technical jargon is minimized
  - Context is provided where needed
  - Not assuming prior knowledge
  - Clarity over perfection

- [ ] **Problem connects to business strategy**
  - Why does this matter to the company?
  - How does it relate to strategic priorities?
  - What's at stake if not addressed?
  - Why now?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
PROBLEM STATEMENT:
We face a strategic inflection point. Our enterprise-focused
positioning has been highly successful ($15M ARR, 95% retention),
but it's left a significant market opportunity on the table. The SMB
segment we're not serving ($2B market, 25% growth) is being captured
by competitors with lower-cost, simpler offerings. Meanwhile, churn
in our existing SMB customers ($5M ARR) is running at 15% annually‚Äî
double our enterprise rate‚Äîprimarily due to pricing misalignment.

STRATEGIC SIGNIFICANCE:
This is not a tactical problem (which we could solve with pricing
tweaks). It's a strategic choice about whether we defend our
premium, enterprise-only positioning or leverage our success to
capture a larger market opportunity. The window for response is
limited‚Äîcompetitive positioning suggests 12-18 months before the
SMB market consolidates around fewer players.
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Root cause proof (just indication)
- ‚ùå Complete market analysis
- ‚ùå Multiple perspectives
- ‚ùå Detailed financial impact

---

### 3. Key Findings (Evidence-Based) ‚úì

- [ ] **Major findings are stated clearly**
  - What did analysis reveal?
  - Each finding is specific (not general)
  - Findings are supported by evidence
  - Findings connect to problem statement

- [ ] **Evidence for findings is provided**
  - Data/research backing finding cited
  - Confidence level indicated (proven, strong, moderate, exploratory)
  - Limitations acknowledged if relevant
  - Assumptions stated if used

- [ ] **Findings tell a coherent story**
  - They build on each other logically
  - Together they explain the situation
  - They point toward decision needed
  - They're not random facts

**What This Looks Like:**
```
KEY FINDINGS:

1. SMB CHURN IS DRIVEN BY PRICING MISALIGNMENT (High Confidence)
   Finding: SMB customers churn because we're 25-30% more expensive
   than primary competitors; exit interviews showed price cited as
   primary reason in 80% of churned accounts.
   Evidence: Internal churn data (CRM); 15 exit interviews;
   competitive pricing analysis
   Confidence: HIGH (internal data validated by multiple sources)

2. ENTERPRISE SEGMENT IS HIGHLY STABLE (High Confidence)
   Finding: Enterprise customers are highly satisfied (NPS 75+)
   and very loyal (95% retention); they value advanced features
   and support quality over cost.
   Evidence: Internal retention/NPS data; enterprise customer interviews
   Confidence: HIGH (clear trend in data)

3. SMB MARKET OPPORTUNITY IS SUBSTANTIAL (Moderate Confidence)
   Finding: SMB addressable market is ~$2B; market is growing at
   25% annually; competitors are consolidating around simplified
   feature sets.
   Evidence: Third-party market research (18 months old);
   customer feedback; competitive analysis
   Confidence: MODERATE (secondary research; may have aged)

4. COMPETITIVE WINDOW IS TIME-LIMITED (Moderate Confidence)
   Finding: Competitors are establishing SMB presence aggressively;
   market may consolidate within 12-18 months, making entry harder.
   Evidence: Competitive monitoring; customer feedback about
   competitor offers; market trends
   Confidence: MODERATE (based on pattern observation, not definitive)
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Exhaustive findings list
- ‚ùå Complete supporting data
- ‚ùå Perfect confidence levels
- ‚ùå All methodologies detailed

---

### 4. Strategic Implications & Insights ‚úì

- [ ] **"So what?" is clearly articulated**
  - What do findings mean for strategy?
  - What strategic choices are emerging?
  - What are we learning about market position?
  - What does this suggest about future?

- [ ] **Trade-offs are made visible**
  - What do we gain with each strategic option?
  - What do we give up?
  - What are we choosing between?
  - Why are trade-offs important?

- [ ] **Opportunities are identified**
  - What becomes possible with this understanding?
  - Where is growth opportunity?
  - What competitive advantage emerges?
  - What could we do that competitors can't?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS:

Our analysis reveals a strategic inflection point: we have successfully
dominated the enterprise market, but in doing so, we've become a premium
player with limited ability to capture the adjacent, larger SMB market.
This isn't a problem to be fixed with tactics; it's a strategic choice.

CHOICE POINT 1: Market Focus
- OPTION A: Defend our premium, enterprise-only positioning
  Gain: Simplicity; maximize enterprise profitability; clear focus
  Give Up: $2B market opportunity; future growth; market share

- OPTION B: Pursue dual-segment strategy (enterprise + SMB)
  Gain: Larger market opportunity; growth potential; market leadership
  Give Up: Organizational simplicity; resource split; near-term profitability

OPPORTUNITY:
If we can serve both enterprise and SMB profitably with distinct
offerings, we could double our addressable market and achieve
market leadership position. Enterprise excellence becomes our
strength; SMB value becomes our reach.

COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE:
We have something competitors don't: proven enterprise success
(retention, NPS, product quality). We could leverage that to move
downmarket with confidence‚Äîtaking best practices from enterprise
and adapting them for SMB simplicity/affordability.
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Final recommendation
- ‚ùå Detailed implementation approach
- ‚ùå Complete strategic analysis
- ‚ùå Quantified opportunity sizing

---

### 5. Recommendations or Framing for Decision ‚úì

- [ ] **Clear recommendation OR clear decision framing**
  - If recommending: Option A is recommended because [rationale]
  - If framing decision: Here are the strategic choices and trade-offs
  - Rationale is clear and defensible
  - Recommendation connects to findings

- [ ] **Next steps are articulated**
  - What should happen next?
  - Who decides?
  - What's timeline for decision?
  - What work happens while deciding?

- [ ] **Success criteria are outlined**
  - What does success look like for recommended approach?
  - What metrics matter?
  - What's the target timeframe?
  - How will we measure success?

**What This Looks Like:**
```
RECOMMENDATION:
Pursue a dual-segment strategy with distinct enterprise and SMB
offerings. Rationale: This approach leverages our proven enterprise
strength, captures the substantial SMB opportunity before market
consolidates, and creates defensible market leadership position.

ALTERNATIVE CONSIDERED:
Enterprise-only focus offers simplicity but leaves significant market
opportunity to competitors. Risk: competitors establish SMB dominance;
we become legacy enterprise player.

NEXT STEPS:
1. Executive decision on strategic direction (this month)
2. If approved, detailed planning for dual-segment execution
   (next quarter)
3. Phase 1 execution: Foundation (Months 1-6)
4. Phase 2 execution: Launch and scale (Months 6-18)

SUCCESS METRICS:
- Enterprise: Maintain $15M+ ARR, >90% retention, NPS >75
- SMB: Reach $10M ARR by Year 2, >80% retention
- Combined: Path to $40M ARR by Year 3; profitability >40%
- Competitive: Establish market leadership in both segments
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Detailed implementation plan
- ‚ùå Complete business case
- ‚ùå Financial models
- ‚ùå Organizational design

### 6. Context & Audience Appropriateness ‚úì

- [ ] **Brief is self-contained**
  - Can be read standalone (doesn't require other documents)
  - Sufficient context for someone unfamiliar with history
  - Background provided where needed
  - Acronyms and jargon explained

- [ ] **Appropriate for stated audience**
  - If for executives: Focuses on strategy, decisions, implications
  - If for board: Includes competitive position, risk management
  - If for operations: Addresses implementation implications
  - Tone matches audience level

- [ ] **Length and format appropriate**
  - Executive briefs: 3-5 pages maximum
  - Detailed briefs: 8-10 pages maximum
  - Not padded with unnecessary detail
  - Easily readable (good spacing, clear sections)

**What This Looks Like:**
```
FOR EXECUTIVE TEAM:
3-page brief focusing on:
- Strategic situation
- Key decision needed
- Recommendation with rationale
- Next steps

FOR BOARD:
5-page brief adding:
- Competitive context
- Risk factors and mitigation
- Financial implications
- Governance/decision process

FOR OPERATIONAL TEAMS:
5-7 page brief adding:
- Implementation implications
- Timing and phases
- Resource needs
- Success metrics and tracking
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Multiple versions (one good brief sufficient)
- ‚ùå Perfect customization for each reader
- ‚ùå Exhaustive appendices
- ‚ùå Presentation-ready formatting

---

### 7. Documentation Quality ‚úì

- [ ] **Writing is clear and professional**
  - Grammar and spelling correct
  - Sentences are clear (not convoluted)
  - Paragraphs are well-organized
  - Active voice used where possible

- [ ] **Sources and data are referenced**
  - Where did findings come from?
  - How confident are we in data?
  - Are there caveats or limitations?
  - Can reader trace back to source?

- [ ] **Document is properly formatted**
  - Clear title and date
  - Sections are labeled
  - Key information is highlighted
  - Easy to navigate and skim

**What This Looks Like:**
```
TITLE: SMB Market Entry Strategic Decision Brief
DATE: November 13, 2025
PREPARED BY: Strategic Team
CONFIDENTIALITY: Internal Use Only

SECTIONS:
1. Executive Summary (1 page)
2. Business Situation (1 page)
3. Key Findings (2 pages)
4. Strategic Implications (1 page)
5. Recommendation & Next Steps (1 page)
6. Appendix: Data Sources & Confidence Levels (1 page)

DATA ATTRIBUTION:
All financial metrics sourced from [system name], validated by [role]
All customer feedback sourced from [number] interviews and
[data source], conducted by [team]
All competitive data sourced from [sources], dated [dates]
All market data sourced from [report name], published [date]
```

**What This Does NOT Require:**
- ‚ùå Polished design/formatting
- ‚ùå Perfect writing (good enough is fine)
- ‚ùå Exhaustive citations
- ‚ùå Complete appendices

## Enhancing Criteria (Improving Quality)

These items strengthen the brief but aren't strictly blocking:

- [ ] **Compelling narrative arc**
  - Brief tells a story, not just list facts
  - Reader is drawn through logic
  - Emotional resonance with audience
  - Call-to-action is compelling

- [ ] **Stakeholder perspectives incorporated**
  - What do different stakeholders think about this?
  - How will this affect different groups?
  - Are legitimate concerns addressed?
  - Is there something for everyone?

- [ ] **Visual elements support text**
  - Key data presented in charts
  - Strategic matrix visualized
  - Competitive positioning clear visually
  - Visuals are simple and clear

- [ ] **Scenario planning included**
  - What if we pursue enterprise-only? Outcomes?
  - What if we pursue dual-segment? Outcomes?
  - What if competitors move faster? Impact?
  - What does success look like?

- [ ] **Financial implications sketched**
  - Investment required
  - Revenue potential
  - Profitability timeline
  - ROI indication

## Quality Checks (Before Approving)

Before confirming brief is complete, ask yourself:

1. **Could a busy executive understand this in 20 minutes?**
   - Is it clear what the situation is?
   - Can they see why this matters?
   - Can they understand the recommendation?
   - Would they be ready to decide?

2. **Does the brief stand alone?**
   - Someone unfamiliar with context could understand it?
   - No unexplained references or acronyms?
   - Sufficient background provided?
   - All key information included?

3. **Is the evidence compelling?**
   - Are findings backed by data?
   - Are confidence levels transparent?
   - Are assumptions stated?
   - Would someone trust the analysis?

4. **Is the recommendation clear?**
   - Do you state what you recommend?
   - Is the rationale clear?
   - Is it defensible against alternatives?
   - Are next steps obvious?

## Readiness Assessment

### ‚úÖ Brief is Ready for Use When

All blocking criteria are met:
- Executive summary captures full situation
- Problem/opportunity is clear and specific
- Key findings are evidence-based
- Strategic implications are articulated
- Recommendation or decision framing is clear
- Brief is self-contained and appropriate for audience
- Documentation quality is professional

**The brief can serve as authoritative executive summary.**

### üîÑ Brief Needs More Work When

One or more blocking criteria are unmet:

- **Executive summary still too detailed?**
  - ‚Üí Reduce to 1 page maximum
  - ‚Üí Focus only on essentials
  - ‚Üí Cut supporting detail

- **Problem not clear?**
  - ‚Üí Rewrite problem statement more specifically
  - ‚Üí Quantify the impact
  - ‚Üí Explain why it matters

- **Findings without evidence?**
  - ‚Üí Add data sources
  - ‚Üí State confidence levels

- **Recommendation unclear?**
  - ‚Üí State explicitly what you recommend

  - ‚Üí Adjust level of detail

## Common Issues & Resolution

| Issue | Signal | Resolution |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Too long** | "This is 15 pages" | Cut to 5 pages max; move detail to appendix |
| **Too vague** | "I don't understand the problem" | Add specific numbers, examples, context |
| **No evidence** | "These are opinions" | Add data sources, confidence levels, caveats |
| **No recommendation** | "What should we do?" | State explicitly; provide rationale |
| **Too technical** | "Non-expert readers lost" | Add context, explain terminology, simplify |
| **Disorganized** | "Not following logic" | Restructure with clear sections; improve flow |

## Post-Validation Actions

Once this checklist passes:

1. **Finalize Brief**
   - Incorporate any final edits
   - Confirm formatting
   - Add any missing citations

2. **Prepare for Presentation**
   - Identify presentation audience
   - Create talking points
   - Prepare for Q&A

3. **Plan Distribution**
   - Who needs to receive brief?
   - What's distribution method?
   - Any confidentiality considerations?
   - Communication timing?

4. **Archive for Reference**
   - Save to project records
   - Document decision date
   - Reference for future phases
   - Learning capture
