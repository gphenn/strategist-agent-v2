## Strategic Vision for Checklists

Checklists serve as **quality gates** and **completeness validators** at critical moments in the strategic workflow. They ensure:

1. **Work quality** - Completeness of analysis and thinking
2. **Readiness for transition** - Sufficient progress before moving to next phase
3. **Consistency** - Standardized quality across different strategic engagements
4. **Agent accountability** - Clear criteria for what "done" means

## Checklist Types & When They Apply

### 1. **Readiness Checklists** (Transition Gates)
Validate readiness to move from one phase to the next
- **When used:** End of phase, before handoff to next persona
- **Purpose:** Ensure sufficient progress before escalation
- **Example:** "Ready to move from DEFINE to SPLIT?"

### 2. **Completion Checklists** (Phase Deliverables)
Validate that a phase's core work is complete
- **When used:** Within a phase, confirming deliverables are done
- **Purpose:** Ensure all required outputs have been produced
- **Example:** "Have we completed all ANALYSE phase analysis?"

### 3. **Quality Checklists** (Work Validation)
Validate that work meets quality standards
- **When used:** Before finalizing outputs within a phase
- **Purpose:** Ensure rigor and completeness of analysis
- **Example:** "Is our competitive analysis sufficiently detailed?"

### 4. **Template-Specific Checklists** (Document Validation)
Validate that populated templates meet requirements
- **When used:** After completing a template
- **Purpose:** Ensure all sections are filled with sufficient depth
- **Example:** "Is the Strategic Brief complete and ready for presentation?"

### 5. **Decision Point Checklists** (Critical Junctures)
Validate readiness for major decisions
- **When used:** Before finalizing strategic choices
- **Purpose:** Ensure decisions are well-founded
- **Example:** "Are we ready to commit to this strategic option?"

## Checklist Design Principles

### 1. **Action-Oriented**
- Use active verbs: "Have we...", "Is the...", "Have you..."
- Each item should be testable and observable
- Avoid vague criteria

### 2. **Graduated Complexity**
- Basic tier: Foundational requirements
- Advanced tier: Depth and rigor requirements
- Expert tier: Excellence and innovation standards

### 3. **Linked & Referenced**
- Each checklist should reference the relevant task, template, or framework
- Create clear connections to strategic methodology phases
- Document what "done" means in context

### 4. **Honest About Minimums**
- What's truly required (blocking)
- What's nice to have (enhancing)
- What can be added later (deferrable)

### 5. **User-Centric**
- Written for both agents and humans
- Include rationale for each item
- Explain consequences of gaps

## Checklist Maintenance

### When to Update Checklists
1. After completing strategic engagements - validate checklist effectiveness
2. When adding new templates - create associated validation checklist
3. When refining tasks - update associated quality checklist
4. When processes change - update phase completion checklists

### Feedback Loop
- Track which checklist items are most frequently "not met"
- Identify patterns where checklists fail to catch issues
- Refine criteria based on real-world usage
- Share learnings across different strategic engagements

## Success Metrics

A successful checklist system will:

1. **Reduce rework** - Catch issues before handing off
2. **Improve consistency** - Similar quality across engagements
3. **Accelerate handoffs** - Clear transition criteria
4. **Build confidence** - Structured validation reduces uncertainty
5. **Enable learning** - Track patterns and improve processes
